	\section*{Preface}
	\label{sec:preface}
	This document describes the methods used to calculate morphometric parameters from three-dimensional medical images.
	Since there exist more than 20 different coordinate systems for three-dimensional medical images, we will define a standard coordinate system for the following calculations.
	Using the established lettering system 'L/R', 'I/S', and 'A/P' for the image axes, we define the coordinate system as 'IPR', i.e.
	\begin{itemize}
		\item the x-axis is the transversal axis, with indices increasing from superior to inferior,
		\item the y-axis is the coronal axis, with indices increasing from anterior to posterior, and
		\item the z-axis is the sagittal axis, with indices increasing from left to right.
	\end{itemize}

	\section{3D CCD}
	\label{sec:ccd}
	In clinical practice, the CCD angle is usually measured in two-dimensional space, i.e. on X-ray images or single coronal CT/MRI slices.
	However, high-resolution three-dimensional imaging offers the opportunity to measure the CCD angle "as is", i.e. how the femur presents in three-dimensional space.

	\subheading{Axes Definition}

	\noindent The CCD angle is defined as the angle between the femoral neck axis and the femoral shaft axis.

	\paragraph{The Neck Axis}
	is an axis that starts at the centre of the femoral head and goes through the centre of the femoral neck.
	\textbf{Algorithm \ref{alg:fhc}} can be used to find the centre of the femoral head.

	\begin{algorithm}[H]
		\caption{Femoral Head Centre Definition}
		\label{alg:fhc}
		\begin{algorithmic}[1]
			\Procedure{FindFemoralHeadCentre}{M}
			\State $M \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Femur}$
			\State $P \gets \{\emptyset\}$
			\State $mpl \gets \underset{x}\min \{(x, y, z) \in \Z \: | \: M[x,y,z] = 1\}$
			\State $c \gets centroid(M[mpl])$
			\State $i \gets mpl$
			\Repeat
				\State $P \gets P \cup contour\_points(M[i])$
				\State $i \gets i + 1$
			\Until
				$M[i, c_y, c_z] = 0$
			\State $r,\: c \gets sphere\_fit(P)$
			\State \Return $r,\: c$
			\EndProcedure
		\end{algorithmic}
	\end{algorithm}

	\noindent A three-dimensional segmentation masks $M$ serves as the basis for the algorithm.

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item In a first step, the most proximal image slice of the femur $mpl$ is determined by finding the segmented voxel with the smallest $x$ coordinate, i.e. the most proximal voxel of the femur.
		\item The centroid of the most proximal layer of the femur (and as such the most proximal voxel of the femoral head), $c$, is calculated and used as a reference point for finding the most inferior point of the femoral head.
		\item The algorithm then iterates through the layers of the femur, starting from the most proximal layer $mpl$, and collects all contour voxels of the femoral head ($P$).
		\item The algorithm stops when the most inferior voxel of the femoral head is reached, i.e. when a layer $i$ is found where the voxel $(i, c_y, c_z)$ with the same y and z coordinates as the aforementioned centroid $c$ is not segmented.
		In other words, the algorithm continues until it can find no more segmented voxel directly inferior to the centroid $c$ of the most proximal layer.
		\item A sphere with radius $r$ and centre $c$ is then fitted to the collected contour points $P$ with a least-squares approach to approximate the femoral head.
	\end{enumerate}

	\begin{figure}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/fhc_sphere_fit}
		\caption{Femoral head approximation by a fitted sphere. Outline voxels of the femoral head in blue, fitted sphere in red.}
		\label{fig:femoral_head}
	\end{figure}

	The centre $c$ of this sphere is the approximate centre of the femoral head (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:femoral_head}}).
	Centre and radius of the fitted sphere can then be used to find the centre of the femoral neck.
	\textbf{Algorithm \ref{alg:fnc}} can be used for this purpose.\bigskip

	\begin{algorithm}[H]
		\caption{Femoral Neck Centre Definition}
		\label{alg:fnc}
		\begin{algorithmic}[1]
			\Procedure{FindFemoralNeckCentre}{M}
			\State $M \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Femur}$
			\State $r,\: c \gets FindFemoralHeadCentre(M)$
			\State $F \gets \{(x, y, z) \in \Z \: | \: M[x, y, z] = 1\}$
			\State $S \gets \{(x, y, z) \in \Z \: | \: dist((x, y, z), c) \geq r \land dist((x, y, z), c) \leq 1.2 \cdot r\}$
			\State $S \gets \{s \in S \: | \: s_z < c_z \land s_x > c_x\}$
			\State $N \gets F \cap S$
			\State $nc \gets centroid(N)$
			\State \Return $nc$
			\EndProcedure
		\end{algorithmic}
	\end{algorithm}

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item On the basis of the femoral head centre with centre $c$ and radius $r$, a hollow sphere is defined around the femoral head with an inner radius of $1.1 \cdot r$ and outer radius of $1.2 \cdot r$.
		A hollow sphere is a type of solid sphere (think, e.g., a cannonball) with an inner and outer radius; its voxels lie between the inner and outer radius, i.e. it's hollow (like an apricot with its seed removed, see \textbf{Figure \ref{fig:apricot}}).
		\item The points $S$ of the hollow sphere are filtered to exclude everything superior and medial to the femoral head centre (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:neck_candidaes}}).
		\item The intersection $N$ of those points and the femur ($S \cap F$) are an approximation of the voxels comprising the femoral neck.
		\item From these voxels $N$, the centre of the femoral neck can be calculated as the centroid $nc$.
		\item The femoral neck axis is then defined as the line from the femoral head centre to the femoral neck centre (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:neck_axis}}).
	\end{enumerate}

	\noindent Since both reference points are represented in vector format, the reference line is simply the vector connecting the two points: \[fna = c - nc\]

	\end{figure}

	\begin{figure}

		\label{fig:neck_axis}
	\end{figure}

	\paragraph{The Shaft Axis}
	is an axis through the centre of the femoral shaft.
	\textbf{Algorithm \ref{alg:fsa}} can be used to get the femoral shaft axis.

	\begin{algorithm}[H]
		\caption{Femoral Shaft Axis Definition}
		\label{alg:fsa}
		\begin{algorithmic}[1]
			\Procedure{GetFemoralShaftAxis}{M}
				\State $M \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Femur}$
				\State $mdl \gets \underset{x}\max \{(x,y,z) \in \Z \: | \: M[x,y,z] = 1\}$
				\State $c_1 \gets centroid(M[mdl])$
				\State $l \gets mdl - k$
				\State $c_2 \gets centroid(M[l])$
				\State $fsa \gets c_1 - c_2$
				\State \Return $fsa$
			\EndProcedure
		\end{algorithmic}
	\end{algorithm}

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item The algorithm starts by finding the most distal layer $mdl$ of the femur analogously to algorithm \ref{alg:fhc}.
		\item A layer $n$ layers superior to the most distal layer, where $n$ is dependent on the voxel spacing, is selected for the starting point of the femoral shaft axis.
		This is done to improve robustness against segmentation errors.
		\item The first starting point of the axis is therefore the centroid $c_1$ of the $(mdl - n)$th slice.
		\item A second layer of the femoral shaft is defined $k$ layers superior to this slice, where $k$ again depends on the transversal spacing of the image.
		\item The centroid $c_2$ of this second layer serves as the second reference point.
	\end{enumerate}

	The femoral shaft axis is then defined as the line from the first through the second reference point (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:shaft_axis}}): \[fsa = c_1 - c_2\]

	\begin{figure}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/shaft_axis}
		\caption{The femoral shaft axis (yellow) from $c_1$ through $c_2$. Ellipses are illustrations of the selected layers $mdl$ and $l$ (depiction not accurate).}
		\label{fig:shaft_axis}
	\end{figure}

	\subheading{Angle Calculation}

	\noindent With both axes defined, the calculation of the CCD angle is trivial, i.e. the angle between both axes (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:ccd}}).
	As mentioned earlier, both axes are in vector form.
	Therefore, we can simply calculate the angle between these two vectors: \[CCD = \arccos\left(\frac{fna \cdot fsa}{\|fna\| \cdot \|fsa\|}\right)\]

	\begin{figure}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/ccd}
		\caption{The CCD angle is defined as the angle between the femoral neck axis (yellow) and the femoral shaft axis (green).}
		\label{fig:ccd}
	\end{figure}

	\section{3D Alpha Angle}
	\label{sec:alpha}
	The alpha angle is defined as the angle between the femoral neck axis and the line ("alpha axis") from the femoral head centre to the point where the femoral head is no longer spherical ("alpha point").
	While on a two-dimensional image slice, there are only two possible alpha points (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:alpha_angle_2d}}), there are infinite possible points in three a three-dimensional volume (i.e. the circumference of the transition between femoral head and neck).

	\begin{figure}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/alpha_angle}
		\caption{Alpha angle measurement in two-dimensional space. Femoral neck axis in blue, alpha axis in green.}
		\label{fig:alpha_angle_2d}
	\end{figure}

	\begin{figure}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/alpha_angle_3d}
		\caption{Alpha angle measurement in three-dimensional space. Femoral neck axis in green, alpha axes ($n=4$) in green.}
		\label{fig:alpha_angle_3d}
	\end{figure}

	\subheading{Axes Definition}

	\noindent The femoral head centre and neck axis are defined as in section \ref{sec:ccd}.

	\paragraph{Alpha Points and Alpha Angle (Algorithm \ref{alg:alpha_angle})}

	\begin{algorithm}[H]
		\caption{Alpha Angle Calculation}
		\label{alg:alpha_angle}
		\begin{algorithmic}[1]
			\Procedure{CalculateAlphaAngle}{fhc,fna,NP}
				\State $fhc \gets \text{femoral head centre}$
				\State $fna \gets \text{femoral neck axis}$
				\State $NP \gets \text{Voxels comprising the femoral neck}$
				\State $P \gets \text{SampleAlphaPoints(NP, n)}$
				\State $\mathrm{A} \gets \emptyset$
				\ForAll{$p_i \in P$}
					\State $\alpha_i \gets \arccos\left(\frac{fna \cdot (p_i - fhc)}{\|fna\| \cdot \|p_i - fhc\|}\right)$
					\State $\mathrm{A} \gets \mathrm{A} \cup \alpha_i$
				\EndFor
				\State $\alpha \gets \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_i \in \mathrm{A}$
				\State \Return $\alpha$
			\EndProcedure
		\end{algorithmic}
	\end{algorithm}

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item We sample $n$ alpha points $P$ on the circumference of the transition from femoral head to femoral neck (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:alpha_angle_3d}}).
		\item For each alpha point $p_i \in P$, we calculate the alpha angle $\alpha_i$ between the femoral neck axis and the line from the femoral head centre through $p_i$.
		\item Finally, we can calculate the mean alpha angle $\bar{\alpha}$ as the average of all alpha angles $\alpha_i \in \mathrm{A}$:
	\end{enumerate}

	\[
		\alpha = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_i
	\]

	\section{Acetabular Anteversion}
	\label{sec:acetabular_anteversion}
	Anomalous acetabular anteversion is a risk factor for hip dysplasia and femoroacetabular impingement.

	\subheading{Axes Definition}

	\begin{figure}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/acetabular_anteversion}
		\caption{Acetabular anteversion measurement in two-dimensional space. Axis $g$ connecting femoral head centres $M$ in green, axis $s$ perpendicular to $g$ through posterior acetabular rim $P$ in orange, axis $t$ connecting $P$ and anterior acetabular rim $A$ in pink.}
		\label{fig:acetabular_anteversion}
	\end{figure}

	\noindent Acetabular anteversion is defined as the angle between an axis $t$, which is perpendicular to a line $g$ connecting both femoral head centres $M$ and goes through the posterior acetabular rim $P$, and an axis $s$, which connects the anterior $A$ and posterior acetabular rim $P$ (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:acetabular_anteversion}}).
	Algorithm \ref{alg:acetabular_anteversion} can be used to calculate the acetabular anteversion.

	\begin{algorithm}
		\caption{Acetabular Anteversion Calculation}
		\label{alg:acetabular_anteversion}
		\begin{algorithmic}[1]
			\Procedure{GetFemoralHeadConnector}{M}
				\State $M \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Femur}$
				\State $c_{left} \gets FindFemoralHeadCentre(M, left)$
				\State $c_{right} \gets FindFemoralHeadCentre(M, right)$
				\State $l \gets (c_{left}.x + c_{right}.x) \: / \: 2$
				\State $g \gets c_{right} - c_{left}$
				\State \Return $g, l$
			\EndProcedure
			\Procedure{GetAnteriorAcetabularRim}{M, s}
				\State $M \gets \text{A 2D Segmentation Mask of the Acetabulum}$
				\State $s \gets \text{Image side}$
				\State $P \gets \{(y, z) \in Z \: | \: M[y, z] = 1 \land y < shape_y(M) \: / \: 2\}$
				\State $P_z \gets \{p_z \in P\}$
				\State $i \gets
					\begin{cases}
						\argmin{i} \: i \mapsto P_z[i] & \text{if} \: s = left \\
						\argmax{i} \: i \mapsto P_z[i] & \text{if} \: s = right
					\end{cases}
				$
				\State \Return $P[i]$
			\EndProcedure
			\Procedure{GetPosteriorAcetabularRim}{M, s}
				\State $M \gets \text{A 2D Segmentation Mask of the Acetabulum}$
				\State $s \gets \text{Image side}$
				\State $P \gets \{(y, z) \in Z \: | \: M[y, z] = 1\}$
				\State $P_z \gets \{p_z \in P\}$
				\State $i \gets
					\begin{cases}
						\argmin{i} \: i \mapsto P_z[i] & \text{if} \: s = left \\
						\argmax{i} \: i \mapsto P_z[i] & \text{if} \: s = right
					\end{cases}
				$
				\State \Return $P[i]$
			\EndProcedure
			\Procedure{GetAcetabularAxes}{$M_A, M_F$, s}
				\State $M_A \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Acetabulum}$
				\State $M_F \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Femur}$
				\State $side \gets \text{Image side}$
				\State $g, l \gets GetFemoralHeadConnector(M_F)$
				\State $a \gets GetAnteriorAcetabularRim(M_A[l], s)$
				\State $p \gets GetPosteriorAcetabularRim(M_A[l], s)$
				\State $s \gets a - p$
				\State $M_s \gets split\_mask(M_F, side)$
				\State $u \gets FindFemoralHeadCentre(M_s)$
				\State $t \gets ((p - u) \cdot g \: / \: g \cdot g) \cdot g + u$
				\State \Return $s, t$
			\EndProcedure
		\end{algorithmic}
	\end{algorithm}

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item We find the femoral head centres $c_{left}$ and $c_{right}$ of the left and right femoral head, respectively.
		\item The femoral head connector $g$ is then defined as the vector connecting both femoral head centres: $g = c_{left} - c_{right}$.
		\item Next, we find the anterior and posterior acetabular rim points $a$ and $p$.
		\item We can find the anterior point $a$ by restricting the search space to the anterior half of the image and selecting the most lateral segmented pixel of the acetabulum.
		\item Since the posterior point is also the most lateral point, we can find $p$ by selecting the most lateral pixel of the acetabulum.
		\item The acetabular axis $s$ is then defined as the vector connecting the anterior and posterior acetabular rim points: $s = a - p$.
		\item We then split the femoral head mask $M_F$ into left and right sides $M_s$.
		\item The acetabular anteversion axis $t$ is then defined as the line from the femoral head centre $u$ through the posterior acetabular rim point $p$ and perpendicular to the femoral head connector $g$. This is done by projecting the vector from $p$ to $u$ onto $g$ and adding the result to $u$. The acetabular anteversion axis is then defined as $t = ((p - u) \cdot g \: / \: g \cdot g) \cdot g + u$.
	\end{enumerate}

	\subheading{Angle Calculation}

	\noindent The acetabular anteversion is then calculated as the angle between $s$ and $t$: \[AA = \arccos\left(\frac{s \cdot t}{\|s\| \cdot \|t\|}\right)\]

	\section{Center-Edge Angle}
	\label{sec:cea}
	\noindent The center-edge angle is a measure of acetabular coverage and is used to diagnose hip dysplasia.

	\subheading{Axes Definition}

	\noindent We need to define three axes to calculate the center-edge angle (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:center_edge}}):
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item A line $g$ connecting left and right femoral head centres $c_l$ and $c_r$
		\item A line $a$ perpendicular to $g$, starting at the femoral head centre $c_{l/r}$ and going in proximal direction
		\item A line $b$ starting at the femoral head centre $c_{l/r}$ and going through the most lateral point of the acetabulum $e$
	\end{enumerate}

	\noindent\textbf{Algorithm \ref{alg:cea}} can be used to calculate the center-edge angle.

	\begin{algorithm}[H]
		\caption{Center-Edge Angle Calculation}
		\label{alg:cea}
		\begin{algorithmic}[1]
			\Procedure{GetCenterEdgeAxes}{F,A}
				\State $F \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Femur}$
				\State $A \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Acetabulum}$
				\State $c_l, c_r \gets FindFemoralHeadCentre(F)$
				\State $g \gets c_r - c_l$
				\State $d = (-1, 0, 0)$
				\State $n = \frac{g}{\lvert \lvert g \rvert \rvert}$
				\State $d_{perp} = d - ((d \cdot n)n)$
				\State $a = c_{l/r} + d_{perp}$
				\State $e \gets FindMostLateralPoint(A)$
				\State $b \gets e - c_{l/r}$
				\State \Return $a, b$
			\EndProcedure
		\end{algorithmic}
	\end{algorithm}

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item We find the femoral head centres $c_l$ and $c_r$.
		\item The femoral head connector $g$ is then defined as the vector connecting both femoral head centres: $g = c_l - c_r$.
		\item We define a direction vector $d$ pointing in the proximal direction.
		\item We calculate the unit vector $n$ in the direction of $g$.
		\item The perpendicular vector $d_{perp}$ to $g$ is then defined as the vector from $d$ to the projection of $d$ onto $n$: $d_{perp} = d - ((d \cdot n)n)$.
		\item The line $a$ is then defined as the vector from the femoral head centre $c_{l/r}$ in the direction of $d_{perp}$: $a = c_{l/r} + d_{perp}$.
		\item We find the most lateral point $e$ of the acetabulum.
		\item The line $b$ is then defined as the vector from the femoral head centre $c_{l/r}$ to the most lateral point $e$: $b = e - c_{l/r}$.
	\end{enumerate}

	\begin{figure}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/center_edge}
		\caption{Center-edge angle in three-dimensional space. Femur in green, acetabulum in blue. Line connecting femoral head centres $g$ in yellow, line $a$ perpendicular to $g$ from $c_{l/r}$ in proximal direction in red, line $b$ from $c_{l/r}$ to most lateral point of the acetabulum $e$ in black.}
		\label{fig:center_edge}
	\end{figure}

	\subheading{Angle Calculation}

	\noindent With both axed defined, we can calculate the center-edge angle between $a$ and $b$:
	\[CEA = \arccos\left(\frac{a \cdot b}{\|a\| \cdot \|b\|}\right)\]

	\section{Femoral Antetorsion (Murphy)}
	\label{sec:murphy}
	The femoral antetorsion is the angle between a proximal and distal reference line. In the method described by Murphy et al., the proximal reference line connects the centre of the femoral head and the centre of the base of the femoral neck directly superior to the lesser trochanter (\textbf{Figure \ref{fig:murphy}}).

	The centre of the femoral head can be found using the algorithm described in section \ref{sec:ccd}.
	We can use the following algorithm to find the centre of the base of the femoral neck:

	\begin{enumerate}
		\item We find the image slice with the most lateral extension of the femur.
		This is where the greater trochanter is located.
		\item We restrict the search space to everything inferior to the greater trochanter.
		\item We find the image slice with the most medial extension of the femur.
		This is where the lesser trochanter is located.
		By restricting the search space, we ensure that this isn't the femoral head.
		\item We select the image slice directly superior to the lesser trochanter.
		\item We find the centroid of this image slice.
		This is the centre of the base of the femoral neck.
		\item We connect the femoral head centre to the centre of the base of the femoral neck to define the proximal reference line $p$.
	\end{enumerate}

	\noindent Usually, the distal reference line connects the posterior condyles of the femur at the knee joint.
	Since the knee joint is not included in our dataset, we simply use the sagittal axis as the distal reference line $d$.

	\subheading{Angle Calculation}

	We can calculate the femoral antetorsion as the angle between the proximal and distal reference lines:

	\[FA = \arccos\left(\frac{p \cdot d}{\|p\| \cdot \|d\|}\right)\]
	
	\begin{figure}
		\centering
		\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/PA000001_murphy_right}

Left image: Femur at the level of the femoral head centre in light purple, femur at the level of the base of the femoral neck in dark purple, reference line connecting centre of the femoral head to centre of the base of the femoral neck in red.
		Right image: Femur at the level of the knee joint in yellow, reference line connecting the posterior condyles in red.}

	\section{Mikulicz Line Deviation}
	\label{sec:mikulicz}
	The Mikulicz line is a line connecting the centre of the femoral head and the centre of the upper ankle joint.
	It can be used to diagnose misalignment of the lower limb, namely genu varum and genu valgum.
	This is determined by the deviation of the Mikulicz line from the mechanical axis of the femur, i.e. position of the knee joint relative to the Mikulicz line.
	Normally, the Mikulicz line approximately goes through the centre of the knee joint.
	If the centre of the knee joint lies medial to the Mikulicz line, the patient has genu valgum, and if it lies lateral to the Mikulicz line, the patient has genu varum.

	\subheading{Axes Definition}

	Three landmarks are needed to calculate the Mikulicz line deviation: The centre of the femoral head, the centre of the upper ankle joint, and the centre of the knee joint.
	The femoral head centre is defined as in section \ref{sec:ccd}.
	Algorithm \ref{alg:mikulicz} can be used to calculate the Mikulicz line deviation.

	\begin{algorithm}
		\caption{Mikulicz Line Deviation Calculation}
		\label{alg:mikulicz}
		\begin{algorithmic}[1]
			\Procedure{GetKneeJointCentre}{M}
				\State $M \gets A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Knee Joint$
				\State $P \gets \{(x, y, z) \in \Z \: | \: M[x, y, z] = 1\}$
				\State $c \gets centroid(P)$
				\State \Return $c$
			\EndProcedure
			\Procedure{GetAnkleJointCentre}{M}
				\State $M \gets A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Ankle Joint$
				\State $P \gets \{(x, y, z) \in \Z \: | \: M[x, y, z] = 1\}$
				\State $c \gets centroid(P)$
				\State \Return $c$
			\EndProcedure
			\Procedure{CalculateMikuliczDeviation}{$M_k$, $M_a$}
				\State $M_k \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Knee Joint}$
				\State $M_a \gets \text{A 3D Segmentation Mask of the Ankle Joint}$
				\State $c_{fh} \gets FindFemoralHeadCentre(M)$
				\State $c_{k} \gets GetKneeJointCentre(M_k)$
				\State $c_{a} \gets GetAnkleJointCentre(M_a)$
				\State $m \gets c_{a} - c_{fh}$
				\State $d \gets \lvert \lvert (c_{fh} - c_k) - ((c_{fh} - c_k) \cdot n) \cdot n \rvert \rvert$
				\State \Return $d$
			\EndProcedure
		\end{algorithmic}
	\end{algorithm}
